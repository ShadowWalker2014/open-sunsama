## What is Notion Calendar?

Notion Calendar is a standalone calendar application that Notion launched after acquiring Cron, a popular calendar app known for its speed, keyboard-driven interface, and clean design. Unlike Notion's workspace features, Notion Calendar is completely free—a strategic move to attract users to Notion's ecosystem.

### The Cron Acquisition Story

Cron was an independent calendar app that gained a cult following among developers and power users for its minimal design, blazing-fast performance, and extensive keyboard shortcuts. When Notion acquired Cron in 2022, the goal was clear: create a native calendar experience that integrates with Notion's workspace tools.

The result is Notion Calendar—Cron's speed and design philosophy combined with deep Notion integration. For existing Notion users, this means finally having a proper calendar app that understands their Notion databases and pages.

### How Notion Calendar Works

Notion Calendar connects to your existing calendar accounts (Google Calendar, Outlook, iCloud) and displays them in a unified view. The experience inherits Cron's power-user DNA:

1. **Connect calendar accounts**: Link Google, Outlook, or iCloud calendars
2. **View unified schedule**: See all calendars in one clean interface
3. **Create events quickly**: Keyboard shortcuts enable rapid event creation
4. **Link to Notion**: Connect events to Notion pages and databases
5. **Schedule meetings**: Built-in scheduling links for external attendees

The interface is deliberately minimal—no clutter, no visual noise. Events display cleanly, and keyboard shortcuts let you navigate and create without touching your mouse.

### Notion Calendar Key Features

**Keyboard-First Design**: This is Notion Calendar's defining characteristic, inherited from Cron. Press "c" to create an event, use arrow keys to navigate, and manage your schedule without leaving the keyboard. Power users can accomplish in seconds what takes minutes in traditional calendar apps.

**Notion Integration**: The unique value proposition for Notion users. Link calendar events to Notion pages. See Notion database items with dates on your calendar. When you schedule a meeting, attach it to a relevant project page. This bidirectional connection makes calendar events meaningful beyond the schedule itself.

**Scheduling Links**: Create booking pages that let others schedule time with you. Define availability windows, set buffer times between meetings, and share links—similar to Calendly but built into your calendar app.

**Multi-Calendar Support**: Connect multiple Google Calendar, Outlook, and iCloud accounts. View them unified or toggle specific calendars on and off. Color coding helps distinguish between work, personal, and project calendars.

**Clean Aesthetic**: Notion Calendar inherits both Cron's minimal design and Notion's aesthetic sensibility. The interface is uncluttered, typography is thoughtful, and the overall experience feels premium despite being free.

**Time Zone Support**: Excellent for remote teams. See events in multiple time zones, schedule across time zones with awareness, and avoid the confusion that plagues international scheduling.

### Notion Calendar Pricing

Notion Calendar is **completely free**. No premium tier, no trial period, no feature gating. This aggressive pricing is strategic—Notion wants users in their ecosystem, and a free calendar removes friction. You need a Notion account to use the app, but the Notion free tier works perfectly.

This represents remarkable value: a polished, full-featured calendar app at no cost.

### Notion Calendar Limitations

**No Task Management**: Notion Calendar is purely a calendar. There's no task list, no to-do management, no way to track what you need to accomplish beyond scheduled events. Tasks live in Notion databases—separate tools for separate purposes.

**No Time Blocking Workflow**: While you can create events for work sessions, there's no integrated workflow for turning tasks into [time blocks](/blog/what-is-time-blocking). You must manually create events and remember to update both your task list and calendar when plans change.

**No Daily Planning Process**: Opening Notion Calendar shows your schedule but doesn't guide you through planning your day. There's no morning ritual, no capacity tracking, no intentional decision-making about what deserves your time.

**No Focus Mode**: When it's time to work on something, Notion Calendar can't help. No timer, no Pomodoro support, no distraction reduction. Execution happens elsewhere.

**Notion Ecosystem Dependency**: The deep integration that makes Notion Calendar valuable for Notion users is meaningless if you don't use Notion. Without Notion databases and pages to link to, you're using a nice calendar without its key differentiator.

**Limited External Integrations**: Beyond calendar providers (Google, Outlook, iCloud), Notion Calendar doesn't integrate with productivity tools like Todoist, Asana, Linear, or Jira. Tasks from those systems stay in those systems.

## What is Sunsama?

Sunsama is a daily planning application built on a specific productivity philosophy: intentional, sustainable planning. While Notion Calendar asks "what's on your schedule?", Sunsama asks "what will you deliberately accomplish today?" It's designed for knowledge workers who want to be more intentional about their daily choices.

### How Sunsama Works

The core Sunsama experience is the morning planning ritual—a structured process you complete each day:

1. **Review your calendar**: See existing meetings and commitments
2. **Pull in tasks**: Import tasks from connected tools (Todoist, Asana, Notion, Linear, Trello, Jira, and more)
3. **Set daily intentions**: Consciously choose what you'll accomplish today
4. **Estimate time**: Add duration estimates to each task
5. **Time block your schedule**: Drag tasks onto specific calendar times
6. **Check capacity**: Sunsama warns if you've scheduled more than realistic

Throughout the day, you work through time blocks using focus mode. At day's end, an optional shutdown ritual helps you reflect on accomplishments and roll incomplete tasks forward.

### Sunsama's Key Features

**Guided Daily Planning**: The morning ritual is Sunsama's philosophical foundation. You deliberately choose what matters today rather than reactively working through an endless list. The guided process builds intentionality as a habit over weeks and months.

**Extensive Integrations**: This is where Sunsama excels for multi-tool users. Native two-way sync with Todoist, Trello, Asana, Notion, ClickUp, Jira, Linear, GitHub, Gmail, Slack, and more. Tasks flow from source systems into your daily plan; changes sync back. Work where tasks originate, plan in Sunsama.

**Two-Way Calendar Sync**: Full bidirectional sync with Google Calendar, Outlook, and iCloud. Time blocks you create in Sunsama appear on your external calendar; events show in your planning view. Your calendar becomes an execution plan, not just a meeting schedule.

**Focus Mode**: When working on a task, focus mode expands it to fill your screen with a timer running. Pomodoro support structures deep work sessions. This execution support separates Sunsama from calendar-only tools like Notion Calendar.

**Overplanning Prevention**: Sunsama tracks estimated task durations and warns when you've scheduled more than can realistically be accomplished. This promotes sustainable productivity over burnout-inducing overcommitment.

**Weekly Review**: Beyond daily planning, Sunsama provides weekly review features to assess progress, understand time allocation patterns, and plan the week ahead strategically.

### Sunsama's Limitations

**High Price Point**: At $240/year compared to Notion Calendar's $0, Sunsama represents a significant investment. The methodology must deliver meaningful productivity improvements to justify the cost.

**No Deep Notion Integration**: While Sunsama can sync with Notion databases, the integration isn't as deep as Notion Calendar's native connection. You can pull tasks from Notion, but you don't get the seamless page-linking experience.

**Slower Event Creation**: Unlike Notion Calendar's keyboard-driven speed, Sunsama uses traditional form-based entry. Creating events requires more clicks and less keyboard fluency.

**Learning Curve**: Sunsama's methodology takes time to adopt. The morning ritual requires consistent practice to become habitual. Users expecting immediate productivity gains may be disappointed during the adjustment period.

**Desktop-First Design**: While Sunsama has mobile apps, the experience is optimized for desktop. Mobile planning feels secondary compared to the web and desktop applications.

## The Notion Ecosystem Question

Choosing between Notion Calendar and Sunsama often comes down to ecosystem commitment.

Notion Calendar makes compelling sense if your productivity system centers on Notion:

- **Tasks live in Notion databases**: Your to-do lists, project trackers, and task boards are Notion databases with date properties
- **Notes and documents in Notion**: Meeting notes link to calendar events, project documentation connects to schedules
- **Team collaboration in Notion**: Shared workspaces mean shared understanding of what calendar events relate to

For this user, Notion Calendar's native integration provides genuine value. Clicking on a meeting reveals the attached Notion page with context, action items, and related documents. Calendar events aren't isolated—they're connected to your broader work.

### If You Use Multiple Tools

Many knowledge workers don't consolidate everything into Notion:

- **Tasks in specialized tools**: Todoist for personal tasks, Linear for engineering, Asana for marketing projects
- **Documents scattered**: Google Docs, Confluence, Notion, and local files depending on team and project
- **Communication in various channels**: Slack threads, email conversations, and meeting notes don't live in one place

For this user, Notion Calendar's Notion-first integration is less valuable. Tasks from Linear don't appear. Asana projects don't connect. The calendar becomes isolated from where work actually originates.

Sunsama's extensive integrations address this fragmentation. Pull tasks from any source, plan them together, and execute through a unified interface. The ecosystem agnosticism becomes a feature.
